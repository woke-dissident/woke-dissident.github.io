The Strategies of Wokecraft

My previous post described the general principles for how woke professors advance the goals of Critical Social Justice (CSJ) in universities. This post looks at the specific strategies and techniques that are used. Actual woke strategies need to be seen in light of the concepts developed in the Principles of Wokecraft post. The key concepts are the following. Woke ethos and wokecraft are driven by what amounts to a religious fervor that leads to the identification of oppression that must be opposed in just about all contexts at a university. The woke will try to oppose oppression by attempting an "advance" in almost all situations. A situation is an event where the woke can advance CSJ goals, such as departmental meetings. The forcefulness of an advance is mediated by two opposing tendencies. The Least Amount of Force Necessary (LAFN) principle tempers the forcefulness of any advance. At the same time, the forcefulness used in a situation increases with the perceived importance of the injustice, resistance to the advance, the proportion of woke professors present in a given situation, and the degree to which the CSJ perspective is entrenched. The LAFN principle is used in order to decrease the chance of detection in early-stage entrenchment, and advances need to be seen in the context of long term gains. Advances in early-stage entrenchment will emphasize short-term, small advances with the aim of significant advances in the long term. The strategies described here are organized along a continuum of forcefulness.

Dog whistles to Establish Who's in The Room

Before any technique of opposition can be used, it is necessary to understand the context of a given situation. This requires understanding the site or act of oppression that needs to be opposed, the degree of oppression represented, the amount of resistance to an advance that can be expected, and how many woke professors are present. The site or act of oppression and the degree of oppression that this represents will be known either before the situation, or quickly after it begins by any woke professor. What can be more difficult to establish (if one does not know the other professors) is whether or not they are woke. Even more difficult to establish is whether to expect resistance from any given professor. That is why the first thing that is done is to evaluate who is in the room. In a room of people who don't know each other, this is done through woke dog whistles.

Dog whistles work in two ways. Dog whistles are used actively by someone trying to identify themselves as woke to other woke members in a situation. The most common dog whistle is the word "critical." It can be used in many ways, but often will be used to qualify a description of themselves. For example, a woke professor might say "My main area of research is in Artificial Intelligence, particularly critical aspects of Artificial Intelligence." The words equity, diversity, inclusion, oppression, justice and privilege are also common dog whistles, with the word "intersection" being perhaps the most subtle. As in "My work focuses not only on the challenges faced by innercity youth, but particularly on the intersection of forces that influence their outcomes." The use of dog whistles in this way will instantly establish the speaker as woke to another woke professor. To professors unfamiliar with the Critical Social Justice (CSJ) perspective, these remarks will go unnoticed, except perhaps by seeming a bit odd, as though they are using a lot of unnecessary words to describe what they do. Professors in the STEM fields are the most likely not to recognize these dog whistles and often will consider such language to be opaque but innocuous, if bemusing. Dog whistles are also used passively by listeners. Woke professors trying to establish who in the room is woke will listen carefully for these words.

Insistence upon Crossover Words

This is the first actual (and thereby least forceful) wokecraft strategy -- the insistence on crossover words. Crossover words are non-technical whose commonplace definitions are well understood and believed to be commonly accepted. They're called crossover words because they also have very and radically different CSJ definitions. The insistence on the use of crossover words is one of the most common woke strategies. It is typically used for advances during early-stage entrenchment and often in the form of comments on documents.

An example would be the proposal for a new course to be offered entitled "Public Health in the Developing World." The classic insistence on a crossover word in this context would be for a woke professor to propose changing the name to "Critical Perspectives on Public Health in the Developing World." Non-woke, uninitiated professors might see this suggestion as a bit odd. While they probably wouldn't see the need to include the expression "critical issues," they wouldn't want to go to raise a great deal of fuss over something as seemingly innocuous as the proposed change. Moreover, they wouldn't be opposed to the word critical, since it is associated with critical thinking, typically seen as the most important thing that a university education will impart to students. Another example would be descriptions for a new departmental hire. An example of this would be a new position being proposed in Public Infrastructure Finance. Perhaps the original intention of the proposed hire was to find an expert on how public infrastructure can be paid for, to fill a lack of expertise in the field in the department. The title would be accompanied by a description of expertise and research areas sought, such as:

"The department is seeking a candidate with an expertise in public infrastructure finance in areas such as, but not limited to: social and economic aspects of electrical, sewerage, stormwater, transportation, communication infrastructure, in developed or developing world contexts."

A typical insistence on crossover words would be to suggest the addition of the clause "...including research at the intersection of these areas." Again, uninitiated professors are not likely to understand why it would be necessary to include such a clause, but they likely wouldn't want to make a fuss about something that seemed so innocuous. Moreover, the real reason for the inclusion of the clause (to use it as a Trojan Horse -- see below) will not become apparent for some time.

Liberal, Good-faith Group Decision-making

In liberal good-faith group decision-making everyone in a situation is allowed and encouraged to express their opinions while also expected to consider others' opinions respectfully. Moreover, the respectful expression of disagreement is also expected and encouraged should it be felt. Finally, liberal decision-making emphasizes argumentation, logic and the presentation and evaluation of evidence. In this tradition, there is a tacit tendency towards consensus in decision-making. While consensus is by no means the goal, and indeed disagreement is seen as fundamental to good decision-making, it is nonetheless typically considered to be preferable. In light of this, in normal circumstances, people will naturally try to adjust discussions and decisions to conform to a tacit consensus position.

The woke redefinition of words is extremely powerful because of its use in combination with the rhetorical technique known as the Motte & Bailey strategy.[1] The first description of this technique was by Shackle (2005). The terms Motte & Bailey refer to a form of medieval European castle introduced into England by the Normans. The motte is a well reinforced tower on a mound that is easy to defend, but unpleasant to stay in. The Bailey is the courtyard below the Motte surrounded by a protected ditch and palisade that is less secure and more difficult to defend, but more pleasant to inabit.

The Motte & Bailey strategy involves a proponent who wants to advocate a difficult-to-defend, extreme position (the Bailey). When (or if) the extreme position is challenged, the proponent retreats to an easily defendible and easily acceptable position (the Motte). The key to the strategy is a hidden false equivalency of the extreme and easily acceptable positions. When woke-redefined words are used in this context, the hidden false-equivalency is related to the two definitions of the word in question: the common-place well understood definition, and the extreme definition. It is difficult for the uninitiated to defend against these strategies because of this rhetorical sleight of hand.

For example during a conversation about the legitimacy of a black person discriminating against a white person, a woke person might well say "well, because blacks are oppressed they can't be racist towards oppressor white people." This is using the extreme woke-definition of racism as the Bailey thrust of an argument. An uninitiated person might reasonably respond, "But that's a double standard." The woke proponent may then retreat to the easily-defendable and acceptable definition of racism (the Motte position) and respond something like "What, you don't believe that racism exists?"

The use of woke-redefined words combined with a Motte & Bailey strategy has been extremely effective. The uninitiated are made to feel (and may actually feel so) that by challenging the extreme definition, the are challenging the easily defendable definition, which no (well-meaning) person would do.

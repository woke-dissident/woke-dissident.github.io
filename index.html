Welcome to the Woke Dissident Blog

I'm a professor at a North American university. I've started this website to write about issues related to North American academia that I find important. I'm writing this blog under the pen name Martin Queen. I am writing because I am dismayed about the state of North American universities. I'd like to help people understand what is wrong with them and if they are academics, how they can try to change them. As I begin this blog, I will concentrate on the the increasing dominance of the Critical Social Justice (CSJ) perspective in our universities.

As such, the first item of business will be blogposts related to Critical Social Justice, which I intend to merge together into "A Primer on the Critical Social Justice Perspective for the Uninitiated." The purpose of the primer is to help those who are concerned about the Critical Social Justice perspective, but who are not very familiar with it; those who understand that something is going wrong, but don't have the background to understand and resist the Critical Social Justice perspective takeover of our universities. It is designed, at this stage, for academics in an academic environment, although I believe much can be transferred to other work environments. I focus on academic environments because that is what I know. As such, the content of the website draws on significant research into the Critical Social Justice perspective and movement. It also draws on my experience in academia and how I have seen Critical Social Justice proponents advance their cause within the academy. I welcome any comments on the posts and in particular any suggested additions; of concepts, of posts or of CSJ tactics as I get to those posts.

The goal is to help those concerned to understand the CSJ perspective, what drives it, identify when and how it makes inroads, and how to resist and prevent it from taking over your department, faculty or university. Many would say that it is too late. While there have been many inroads, the takeover until now has been largely in fine arts, the humanities and most of the social sciences. While rolling it back may be difficult in these disciplines, it is not too late to protect the sciences, engineering and business schools.

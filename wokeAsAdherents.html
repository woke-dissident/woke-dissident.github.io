The Woke as Adherents to the CSJ Perspective

This post explains why I'm going to begin use the term "woke." The term woke derives from the Critical Social Justice (CSJ) perspective and first originated there. It's the simple past of the verb wake, and it is intended to mean awoken to the CSJ worldview: that individual humans are defined by the groups they are identified with based upon characteristics such as skin color, sex, or sexual orientation; that society is composed of oppressor and oppressed groups; that oppression is maintained through language and systems of knowledge by oppressor groups to advantage them and disadvantage oppressed groups; that human group members are subject (see the Subject Principle) to the structures of power and behave in such a way so as to perpetuate those existing structures.

What's interesting is that while the term derives from the CSJ perspective and is used proudly as a self-identification, the same term is used pejoratively among those critical of the perspective. It is because of its pejorative connotations that I have been reluctant to use it so far. At the same time, it is difficult to come up with any other term that is so understandable and concise. As such, and even though I am critical of the CSJ perspective and reluctant to use pejorative terms, I will begin using the term from now on. The definition I'll use for "the Woke" is: people who are conscious of the Critical Social Justice perspective and who adhere to it. That they adhere to it is to say also that they agree with it. As such, this is a three-dimensional definition with the dimensions being: knowledge/consciousness of, adherence to, and agreement with the CSJ perspective.

That woke refers to the adherents, knowledgeable of the CSJ perspective, logically excludes those who are not woke. That woke has three dimensions, however, implies up to nine categories. For now I'll only comment on two of these categories since they're the ones to whom I'll make the most reference. I'll refer to dissidents as those who know (or are conscious of) the CSJ perspective but do not adhere to it and are critical of it. I use the word dissident because one characteristic of the perspective itself, and our culture that is increasingly swayed by its norms and rules, is that people are not allowed to question or disagree with it. The current canceling, de-platforming and quashing of dissent with the CSJ perspective closely parallels accounts of the quashing of counter-revolutionary ideology in the Soviet bloc as described by, among others, Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn. As such, the term dissident is appropriate. I'll refer to the uninitiated as those not conscious of the CSJ perspective. They may or may not adhere to it. Because they aren't conscious of it, they can't really be said to agree with or criticize it. In this category is a large proportion of, for example, academics in the STEM fields.
